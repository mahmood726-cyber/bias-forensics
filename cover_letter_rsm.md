Dear Editor,

We submit for your consideration the manuscript **"The Bias Fingerprint: How Eight Publication Bias Methods Agree and Disagree Across 307 Cochrane Meta-Analyses"** for publication in *Research Synthesis Methods*.

**Motivation:** Publication bias threatens the validity of meta-analytic conclusions, and multiple detection and correction methods exist — yet they are almost always applied individually. When applied simultaneously to the same data, do they agree? This question is fundamental to meta-analytic practice but has never been answered at scale.

**What we did:** We applied eight publication bias methods simultaneously — Egger's test, Begg-Mazumdar, p-curve analysis, three-parameter selection models, trim-and-fill, PET-PEESE, selection model correction, and limit meta-analysis — to 307 Cochrane systematic reviews from the Pairwise70 dataset.

**Key findings:**
- Only 17.6% of reviews were classified as Clean across all methods
- 33.6% had confirmed bias (3+ methods agreed), but 13.7% produced discordant fingerprints where correction methods disagreed on the *direction* of bias
- PET-PEESE and limit meta-analysis agreed 98% of the time (mathematically expected), while detection tests (Egger, Begg) agreed only 82.7%
- The mean relative effect shift across correction methods was 1.01 — corrections frequently alter estimates by amounts comparable to the estimate itself

**Relevance to RSM readers:** This work directly addresses the methodological question of which bias method to use — and the answer is: not just one. The 13.7% discordance rate means automated single-method correction is unreliable for one in seven reviews. We advocate transparent multi-method reporting using "bias fingerprints."

This study is a companion to the Fragility Atlas (submitted to *The BMJ*), which found that publication bias correction was the single most influential analytical dimension in multiverse meta-analysis (eta-squared = 0.374).

The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere. Analysis code and data are publicly available.
